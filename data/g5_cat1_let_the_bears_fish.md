## Let the Bears Fish
by Carolyn Short                                  
     
People come to Hyder, Alaska, every summer just as the salmon arrive to lay their eggs on the gravelly stream bottom and die. The bears come to feed on the spawning salmon and to put on the weight they need to survive the winter. The people come to watch the bears. But visitors haven’t always had a safe viewing platform. In the past, there was only a short, unprotected platform. To get to it, people had to walk along the bears’ paths. Sometimes the bears climbed onto the platform. Over the years, the number of visitors grew. They didn’t all fit on the platform, so they wandered everywhere. Since the bears wandered everywhere, too, many visitors were in danger. The Forest Service hired people to keep the visitors and bears apart.  

But there were still problems. Many visitors didn’t realize that bears are wild animals. Some brought food. Others got too close when they took pictures. Some set up chairs on the bears’ paths.  

Watching people watching bears turned into one big headache. What could be done?

How could the Forest Service provide a safe place for bears to fish while people watched? How could they build a new platform that wouldn’t block the bears’ paths? How could they avoid destroying the brush the bears use for cover?

If the area were made safer, more people would come. Where would they park? Photographers worried that if they had to take pictures from a platform, vibrations from people walking would ruin their photographs. The Forest Service came up with a plan. Along the stream, they would build a platform stretching nearly the length of one and a half football fields.  

The platform would be from six to twelve feet off the ground so that bears could walk under it. A wide cap atop the railing would give people a place to lean and a steady base for taking pictures. By winding the platform through the trees, the builders could avoid cutting them down.  

As a landscape architect with the Forest Service, my husband helped design the viewing platform and oversaw its construction.  

Every week, he would return home with tales of the bears he had seen. Our family couldn’t wait to see this place!

In August, when we arrived in Hyder, we drove six miles to the viewing area. It was nearly 7 P.M. when we parked the car and walked along the fenced walkway leading from the new, larger parking lot to the platform.  

About two hundred people had arrived before us. They lined the platform, watching and chatting. Suddenly the crowd hushed. Someone whispered, “It’s The Brat!” I watched as this grizzly caught a big salmon and dragged it up onto the grass.  

The best time to watch the bears is in the early morning and evening, but the next day we arrived in the middle of the day and spotted a black bear walking along the stream. Both grizzly and black bears feed here, but black bears usually come when grizzlies aren’t around. We heard that a well-known grizzly called Aussie was napping near a pond on the other side of the platform. We waited to see if he would appear. Before long a snout pushed through the brush. Aussie stepped off the bank and into the pond. Only his head showed as he paddled. Reaching the muddy shallows, he turned onto his side and batted at a nearby log. Rolling over, he swam toward the old dike road, where people used to gather. Water droplets glistened on his fur as he climbed out and padded down the road.  

Cameras clicked furiously. Turning left, Aussie walked under the platform. I lost sight of him until he waded into the stream. Water sprayed in white plumes as salmon flipped out of his way. Aussie pounced, pinning a salmon against the gravel with his massive paw.  

He carried the squirming salmon in his mouth to the opposite shore. After dropping the salmon in the grass, he pushed down on its belly with one paw. Reddish streams of fish eggs spurted out. Aussie left the salmon and licked the tasty roe off the grass and gravel.  

Aussie continued eating his lunch as dozens of people watched. He never looked up at us looking down at him. Did he know we were there? I don’t know, but I was glad he could fish in peace while we watched in safety from the new viewing platform.

## Battle in the Sky!
by Lisa Rosinsky

High in the air above the National Mall in Washington, D.C., at the foot of the Washington Monument, five huge, brightly colored kites dip and dive. It’s a cloudy, windy day—perfect weather for kite flying. Suddenly, a red-and-black kite darts straight at an American-flag-patterned kite. Their lines are tangled! Down on the ground, the kite flyers begin to saw their lines back and forth, trying to pull each other’s kites out of the sky. “We have engagement!” shouts the announcer. The audience cheers. The 2012 Blossom Kite Festival Rokkaku Battle has begun!

Rokkaku kites are six-sided “fighter kites” and can be very large. Some are even taller than an adult! Rokkaku battles began in Japan around 1649, and many other cultures in Asia and the Middle East have similar kite-fighting customs. Traditional Japanese Rokkaku kites are painted with images of heroes from folklore or history. Modern kites, however, might be decorated with any design, including animals, insects, flags, or funny faces. Included in the battle today are a rainbow kite and a five-foot-tall kite with a picture of a rock star!

The kite flyers are chasing each other around the field, each trying to wrap his or her kite string, or “line,” around someone else’s. One by one, the kites drop out of the battle, until there are only two kites left in the sky. The red-and-black kite belongs to Andrew Albosta. Andrew’s line is tangled around the other kite’s, and he begins to saw his line back and forth. The other kite wavers, then its line breaks! The kite starts floating away, then falls to the ground. When a kite touches the ground or its line is cut, that kite’s team is out. Andrew is the winner!

But the battle is not over yet. Just like a game of rock-paper-scissors, it’s the best out of three. This competition has three “heats,” or rounds. The same five kites compete in each heat. The last kite remaining in the sky at the end of each heat wins points. After all three heats, the judges add all the points together to determine the final winner.

Rokkaku battles can be fought between individuals or teams. For this battle, each team must have four members: a pilot, a copilot, a launcher, and a scorekeeper. The pilot and copilot work together to fly the kite, and the scorekeeper helps keep track of the points. The person who launches the kite is also in charge of retrieving it when it falls. Andrew is the pilot of his team.

Andrew’s team wins the next heat, too! Now there’s just one more round to go. The announcer gives the “Launch!” signal, and the final round begins! The rock-star kite is back, and so is the American-flag kite. It’s a close contest, but Andrew wins a third and final time, making him the first-place winner of the 2012 Blossom Kite Festival Rokkaku Battle.

Andrew has been flying kites since he was a kid and started entering competitions when he was a teenager. He also enjoys building his own kites.

If you’re interested in flying and building kites, you can ask your parents to help you contact a local kite organization or club. You can also find resources at your local library.
